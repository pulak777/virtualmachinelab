reactance [Xq] of a 3 phase synchronous machine by slip test
Objective:
1.
To perform experiment on a salient pole synchronous machine to find out the

a. Direct axis reactance (Xd)
b.
Quadrature axis reactance (Xq)
The Experiment Covers the Course Outcomes:

Basic Theory:
In a 3-phase cylindrical rotor synchronous machine, the air-gap is uniform in between
stator and rotor; hence reactance of the machine remains constant. In a salient pole
machine however, air-gap length varies from minimum to maximum, so reactance
varies from maximum to minimum. These components are taken along (i) the axis of
poles and
(ii) axis quadrature to this. These are also known as direct axis and
quadrature axis respectively. The component of armature MMF acting along the direct
axis has to overcome lesser reluctance as compared to the component of armature
MMF acting along quadrature axis, and can therefore , establish more flux. On the
other hand, the magnetic circuit along quadrature axis path has higher reluctance and,
therefore, quadrature axis MMF will establish lesser flux. Under these conditions we
define two reactances as follows.
Direct axis reactance (along pole axis)
= Xd
Quadrature axis reactance (along inter-polar axis)
= Xq
To aid the analysis a model, known as the ‘two-reaction model” is proposed where the
armature winding is considered to be made up of two coils of equal turns, one located
along direct axis and another located along quadrature axis. One is having reactance
equal to Xd, and the other Xq.
From the slip test, the values of Xd and Xq can be determined. The synchronous
machine is driven by a separate prime mover at a speed slightly different from
synchronous speed. The field winding of the synchronous machine is left open and
balanced three-phase voltages of reduced magnitude are supplied across the armature
terminals. Under these conditions, the relative velocity between the field poles and the
rotating armature MMF wave is equal to the difference between synchronous speed
and the rotor speed i.e. the slip speed. A small AC voltage appearing across the open
field winding indicates that the field poles and the rotating mmf wave are revolving in
the same direction which is required in slip test.
While rotating, the stator flux passes across the air gap, then through the rotor poles
and then back to the stator. Thus, reluctance offered to the flux varies at twice the slip
frequency (for a two pole machine) from maximum to minimum. Reluctance is
maximum when the RMF axis is along the inter-polar axis (q-axis) and the reluctance
is minimum when the RMF axis is directly along the poles (d-axis). Thus, when the
RMF axis is along d-axis, then effective reactance offered by the machine is Xd. On the
other hand, when the RMF axis gets aligned along q-axis, then effective reactance is
Xq. Sincle reluctance along d-axis is much less compared to reluctance along q-axis, it
is obvious that Xd
>> Xq, and therefore the current drawn by the machine when the
RMF axis is aligned with direct axis is lower than when the RMF axis is aligned with
the quadrature axis. Hence, as shown in Fig
5.8.1(b), peak value of the power
frequency sinusoidal current drawn by the armature varies cyclically at twice the slip
frequency with minimum value when machine reactance is Xd and maximum value
when machine reactance is Xq. Due to voltage drop in the supply lines, the voltage
available at armature terminals, as read by the voltmeter, reaches maximum when the
current drawn in minimum, while the voltage reaches minimum value when the
armature current is maximum. Thus, peak value of the power frequency sinusoidal
voltage at the machine terminals also varies cyclically with double the slip frequency,
but in the reverse manner as the current variations as shown in Fig 5.8.1(c).

10)

Experiment No: 9
Title: V-curve of Synchronous motor
Objective:
1.
Perform an experiment to obtain the V-curve of a synchronous motor
The Experiment Covers the Course Outcomes:
Bloom’s Cognitive Domain:
Basic Theory:
V-curve of a synchronous machine shows its performance in terms of variation of
armature current with field current when the load and input voltage to the machine is
constant. When a synchronous machine is connected to an infinite bus, the current
input to the stator depends upon the shaft-load and excitation (field current). At a
constant load, if excitation is changed the power factor of the machine changes, i.e.
when the field current is small (machine is under-excited) the P.F. is low and as the
excitation is increased the P.F. improves so that for a certain field current the P.F. will
be unity and machine draws minimum armature current. This is known as normal
excitation. If the excitation is further increased the machine will become over-excited
and it will draw more line current and P.F. becomes leading and decreases. Therefore,
if the field current is changed keeping load and input voltage constant, the armature
current changes to make VIcosφ constant. Because of their shape (Fig 5.9.1), graphs
of variation of armature current with excitation are called ‘V’ curves.
Fig 5.9.1 V curve of synchronous motor
However, remember that a synchronous machine does not possess starting
torque but once brought near to synchronous speed, it is capable of developing
torque. Synchronous machine can be started either by means of some auxiliary
machine coupled to its shaft or by running it as an induction machine till it attains a
speed near to the synchronous speed.
Usually starting synchronous machines as an induction motor is quite common. In
this case, the field circuit of the synchronous machine is closed through high
resistance (if possible) and reduced voltage is applied across the stator windings. Due
to three-phase balanced supply into the windings of the stator a rotating magnetic
field is set up which rotates at synchronous speed. This rotating field interlinks with
the damper winding (short circuited copper grids placed on the pole shoes), which
behaves just like the squirrel cage rotor of an induction motor. Thus the motor starts
as an induction motor in the direction of rotating field and comes to full speed which
is slightly less than synchronous speed and field circuit of the synchronous machine
can be connected to the D.C. supply. During starting, close the field winding through
a high resistance to reduce the current in the field winding.
The experimental setup is shown in Fig 5.9.2.
Fig 5.9.2 Experimental setup for obtaining V-curve of 3-phase synchronous motor
Procedure:
i)
Make circuit connections as per Fig 5.9.2
ii)
For starting the motor, don’t give any DC supply to the field and connect the
field terminals across a high value resistance through the DPDT switch.
iii) A 3-phase 50Hz AC supply is applied to the stator with the variac position set to
its minimum value.
iv) Gradually, the supply voltage to synchronous motor is increased and then motor
starts running as squirrel cage induction motor.
v) The supply voltage is gradually increased while keeping watch on the armature
current Ia. It will reach a high value and then suddenly will fall to a low value.
vi) Set the variable field supply regulator to 25% position, but do not change the
DPDT switch yet.
vii) At that instant, the DPDT switch connected across the field is now moved to the
other position so the DC supply is applied to the field.
viii) Then the motor is pulled into synchronism and motor now works as a
synchronous motor.
ix) Gradually the supply voltage to stator is increased by observing the armature
current. If Ia increases above the rated value then increase If such that Ia will be
within limits and thus full rated supply voltage is gradually given to the motor.
x) Now motor will work as synchronous motor with full rated voltage.
xi) By varying If in steps in both ways, armature currents Ia are recorded.
